# Pet Medical Care Reference

## Medication Administration
Administer medications at the same time each day for consistent blood levels. Use calendar reminders or a scheduling app to avoid missed doses. Never skip doses without consulting a vet. Store medications as directed — some require refrigeration. For difficult-to-pill pets, ask your vet about liquid formulations or pill pockets.

## Signs of Pain in Pets
Dogs showing pain: limping, reluctance to move, whimpering, changes in posture, aggression when touched, loss of appetite, or excessive licking of one area. Cats showing pain: hiding, changes in grooming habits, hunched posture, reduced activity, changes in litter box use, or facial tension. Pets often hide pain — subtle behavioral changes are often the first sign.

## Dental Health
Dental disease affects 80% of dogs and 85% of cats by age 3. Brush teeth 3x per week (5–10 min) using pet-safe toothpaste — never human toothpaste. Signs of dental disease: bad breath, drooling, pawing at mouth, or difficulty eating. Professional dental cleanings under anesthesia may be required annually for pets prone to tartar buildup.

## Weight Management
Obesity is the most common preventable disease in companion animals. Ideal body condition: ribs palpable but not visible, a visible waist when viewed from above. If your pet is overweight, reduce daily portions by 10–15% and increase activity gradually. Avoid table scraps and measure food rather than free-feeding.

## Vaccination Schedule
Core vaccines for dogs: Rabies, DHPP (distemper, adenovirus, parvovirus, parainfluenza). Core vaccines for cats: Rabies, FVRCP (herpesvirus, calicivirus, panleukopenia). Boosters required every 1–3 years depending on the vaccine type, local regulations, and individual risk factors.

## Parasite Prevention
Heartworm prevention: monthly oral or topical medication, year-round even in cold climates. Flea and tick prevention: monthly topical, oral chewable, or long-acting collar. Intestinal parasites: annual fecal test recommended; deworm as needed. Year-round prevention is more cost-effective and protective than seasonal treatment.

## Senior Pet Medical Care
Pets age faster than humans. A 7-year-old dog is roughly equivalent to a 44–56 year-old human. Semi-annual vet visits are recommended for pets aged 7+. Common senior conditions to monitor: arthritis (reduced mobility, difficulty with stairs), kidney disease (increased thirst and urination), dental disease, cognitive dysfunction, and cancer. Early detection dramatically improves outcomes and quality of life.
