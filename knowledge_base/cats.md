# Cat Care Guide

## Feeding
Cats are obligate carnivores and need high-protein food. Feed adult cats 2–3 times daily (5–10 min per feeding). Kittens under 6 months need 3–4 meals per day. Measure portions carefully — cats are highly prone to obesity. Always provide fresh water; many cats prefer running water, so a fountain may increase intake.

## Litter Box Maintenance
Clean litter boxes daily (5–10 min) — cats avoid dirty boxes and may eliminate elsewhere if they are not kept clean. Fully replace litter and scrub the box weekly (15–20 min). One litter box per cat plus one extra is the recommended rule. Place boxes in quiet, accessible locations.

## Grooming
Short-haired cats: brush weekly (10 min) to reduce shedding. Long-haired cats: daily brushing (15–20 min) prevents hairballs and painful mats. Nail trimming every 2–3 weeks (10 min). Most cats do not need baths unless they get into something. Ear cleaning monthly if wax accumulates.

## Enrichment and Play
Cats need mental stimulation to prevent boredom and stress. Interactive play with wand toys is recommended twice daily (10–15 min each session). Window perches, cat trees, and puzzle feeders enrich the indoor environment. Without stimulation, cats develop anxiety, aggression, or destructive behavior.

## Medical Care
Adult cats need annual wellness vet visits. Dental disease affects 85% of cats over 3 years — dental brushing or dental treats significantly reduce risk. Watch weight: obesity is the most common preventable health problem in cats. Indoor cats need annual exams; outdoor cats may need more frequent parasite checks.

## Special Needs: Thyroid Medication
Hyperthyroidism is common in cats over 10 years old. Medication (methimazole) is typically given twice daily, 12 hours apart, at consistent times. Administer at the same times each day to maintain stable blood levels. Monitor for side effects: vomiting, scratching at face, lethargy, or loss of appetite. Regular blood tests (every 3–6 months) are needed to monitor thyroid levels and kidney function.

## Special Needs: Senior Cats
Cats aged 11+ years are considered seniors. They benefit from more frequent vet visits (every 6 months), joint-friendly low furniture, and warmer sleeping spots. Kidney disease is the leading cause of death in senior cats — increased drinking and urination are early warning signs. Cognitive dysfunction syndrome (cat dementia) may cause nighttime yowling and disorientation.
